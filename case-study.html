touch the water
The Brief

"The brief wasn't for guests to visit a website. It was for them to feel like they'd arrived somewhere."

That was the whole brief, and the founder set it for herself. She was planning her own wedding, at dusk, by water, and every site she found on the market was the same shape wearing different colors: a hero photo, a countdown, a form. Pretty, in the way anything competently assembled is pretty, but none of it felt like arriving anywhere.

So she stopped looking for a template and started asking a stranger question: what does a wedding actually feel like in the moment just before it begins? For her, it was stillness before movement: the surface of water an instant before something disturbs it. That became the entire site.

The Concept

The site's first and only real trick is that it does nothing until you touch it. No autoplay, no scroll prompt, no arrow nudging you forward. Just still water, and the quiet understanding that something will happen when you reach toward it, because that's what water has always done.

One touch, one ripple, radiating outward at the unhurried pace of something with real weight behind it. Not a UI animation. A disturbance. The names appear only once the water has finished settling again, the way a reflection resolves after you've thrown a stone.

one point of contact, unhurried outward motion
The Build

Once the water was right, the rest of the site was built to earn the same quiet, every piece doing one job, nothing competing for attention at once.

Arrival

The chapter scroll

The couple's story told in four unhurried movements rather than one long paragraph, each chapter given its own held moment before the next begins.

Journey

The crossing

Guests move a single vessel across open water by hand to uncover the day's logistics. Arrival, not information, is the framing.

Together

The living wall

A photo wall that fills in real time as guests add to it. It's the only part of the site that keeps changing after the wedding is over.

Presence

One quiet countdown

No urgency, no red digits. Time counted the way you'd count it yourself, waiting for something you're looking forward to.

The Reveal

It was never really about the water.
It was about building something that finally felt like hers.
